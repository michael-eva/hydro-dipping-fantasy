What is Hydro-dipping?

Hydro-dipping, also known as liquid-dipping, water-transfer printing and hydro-graphics, is the process of applying a printed design onto any paintable surface using water as the transfer medium. As a rule of thumb, if the item can be dipped in water and can be painted using traditional painting techniques then the item can be hydro-dipped.

How does it work?

The item first needs to be prepared before it can be hydro-dipped. The surface must be cleaned and primed and depending on the design of the film you are using, it might need a base coat before it can be hydro-dipped. Some film designs are clear which means that the base coat you choose will affect the outcome.

A container of water is needed which will be large enough to fit the object comfortably in and deep enough so that the whole item be submerged underwater.

The film needs to be cut to the correct size to ensure that no film is wasted. It is best to leave about 5cm extra on the sides. If you are starting out, it might be easier to cut a bigger piece than you need just to get used to the process.

It is important to use masking tape to mark off a box in the container around the film about 3cm bigger than the film because in the steps to follow, the film will expand and we can't let it expand too far or else it will distort the design.

The film has to be placed into the container being sure to place it the right way around. If you lick your thumb and index finger and grab onto the corner of the film, the side of the film that sticks to your finger goes face down into the water. The most effective way to put the film into the water is by grabbing one corner and the corner diagonal to it and gently lowering it into the container so that no air bubbles get trapped under the film. If air bubbles happen to get trapped, gently work them out or else it will affect the design when the item gets dipped.

The film needs to sit in the water for at least a minute so that the film can soak up the water. If you leave it for more than a minute, you will achieve the same results but if it's left for less than a minute, it might not work the way it's supposed to. If you watch the film, you will see that it will become wrinkly and a few moments after that it will become really taut. Once it has become taut, it's ready to have activator sprayed on it.

This is the tricky part because all films are different and each film will react differently to the activator. The 3 main outcomes are:

  • The film liquifies perfectly,
  • The film doesn't seem to liquify, or
  • The film liquifies too much.

When the film liquifies perfectly:

The film will have a glassy look to it when it liquifies perfectly. The film will become almost the same consistency as the water and when you dip your item in, the film won't wrap around your item as it would if the film didn't liquify properly.

When the film doesn't seem to liquify

Some film have such a strong PVA base that they won't liquify as expected no matter how much activator gets sprayed on. You are still able to dip your item, it will just be a bit more difficult and you'll have to tape up any surfaces you don't want painted.

The film liquifies too much

I personally have not experienced this (yet), but I have been doing some research and when the film liquifies too much, the consistency will be similar to when it liquifies perfectly, but you will see that the design becomes very faded until there isn't a design anymore. The way to get around this is to use less activator.

At this stage, I'm still trying to find suppliers who supply consistent film which will always liquify perfectly, until then, I will add an extra 50cm of film to your order so that you can do a bit of testing for yourself and see what works best.

After the film has liquified it's time to dip your item. Be sure to tape up any parts of the item you don't want dipped. It's also very helpful to use the tape to fashion a handle onto the back of the item so that the paint is able to cover the whole area.

A slow and steady approach works best being sure to not twist the item as it's being dipped because the design will warp and not look the way you expect it to. The best way to dip the item is at a 45 degree angle. If you want to be creative, twist the design and see what happens, you might like the outcome even more than the original design!

Once the item is fully submerged, gently shake the item from side to side so that the left over film at the top of the container moves to the side. This is to ensure that any leftover film doesn't ruin the design. Then lift the item out of the water and hopefully it looks the way you wanted it to!

The freshly painted item will have a slimy layer over the top of it, you will have to get rid of this layer once it's dry. Leave it out to dry until you are sure the paint is dry, then rinse the item with warm water for about 2 minutes or so to get all the slime off of it.

Once it has dried, it's time to add some glossy clear coat to it to give it a nice smooth and clean finish. If you're feeling adventurous, try any other finishing coat and see what happens. Be sure to let me know what you tried and how it came out, I'd be very interested to see!
